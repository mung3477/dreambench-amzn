You are selecting product subjects for evaluating fine-grained subject identity preservation in reference-based image generation.

Given a set of candidate product images, identify products whose identity depends on fine-grained visual details rather than only on coarse object category, overall silhouette, or dominant color.

Prefer products that satisfy the following properties:

1. **High intra-category visual similarity**
   Products within the same category share a broadly similar overall shape and semantic appearance, making coarse category-level similarity insufficient for distinguishing product identity.

2. **Identity-bearing local details**
   The specific product can be distinguished by localized visual attributes such as:

   * logos, typography, labels, or symbolic marks;
   * characteristic color placement or multi-color composition;
   * surface patterns, textures, material appearance, or finish;
   * stitching, seams, panel boundaries, or decorative elements;
   * small geometric features;
   * the number, shape, position, or arrangement of functional parts;
   * distinctive part-to-part spatial relationships or structural configurations.

3. **Identity impact of small errors**
   Altering, omitting, relocating, or hallucinating a small number of these details would make the generated subject recognizably inconsistent with the reference product, even if the overall object category, pose, shape, and dominant color remain correct.

4. **Visual verifiability**
   The identity-bearing details must be clearly visible and verifiable from the provided reference image. Do not select a product based primarily on hidden specifications, internal functionality, model metadata, or attributes that cannot be visually established.

5. **Beyond text-only dependence**
   Do not prefer products solely because they contain large amounts of readable text. Favor products whose identity can also depend on non-textual fine-grained appearance or structure.

Avoid:

* generic products whose identity is mostly determined by coarse shape or color;
* products with few visually distinctive local attributes;
* products that can only be distinguished through hidden technical specifications;
* visually near-identical commodity products differentiated mainly by seller metadata;
* images where the subject is too small, occluded, blurry, or poorly lit for detailed visual inspection;
* plain, generic, or solid-colored apparel/clothing (such as a plain red vest, a solid black t-shirt, or a plain blue hoodie) that lacks visually distinctive logos, complex patterns, unique textures, or characteristic multi-color compositions.


For each candidate, assess the following counterfactual:

“If the overall object shape, category, pose, and dominant colors were preserved, but several small local details were changed, would the result still clearly represent the same specific product?”

Prefer candidates for which the answer is **No**.

Select products that provide strong test cases for distinguishing semantic similarity from fine-grained product identity preservation.
